Creative strategy is NOT your first step. It becomes critical once you hit a specific point in your journey:

You're getting sales but can't consistently replicate the results. You've had winners but don't know what made them win.

What Is Creative Strategy?

The Decision Layer Before Design

Creative strategy is a decision-making layer that happens before you write copy or design ads. It answers the question: "What ideas should I test?" — not "How should this ad look?"

A lot of people think a nice-looking ad is what converts. It's not. The messaging behind the ad is what converts. Creative strategy unpicks that messaging.

Without Creative Strategy

  • Random testing produces random results
  • You can't isolate why something worked
  • Winning ads can't be repeated or scaled
  • You're chasing luck, not building a system
  • "I don't know what ad to make next"
VS

With Creative Strategy

  • Structured testing with clear variables
  • You know exactly which idea converted
  • Winners can be iterated and scaled
  • Strategy removes guessing from scaling
  • "I know my next 25 ads and why each exists"
The Core Principle: If you've ever been stuck and didn't know what creative to make next, that means you don't have a creative strategy. This system fixes that permanently.
2

Mass Desires (Not Just Problems)

Why Desires, Not Problems?

Problems

Explain what is broken in the customer's life.

Problems determine how aware the customer is and how much context they need. They help you decide how to speak.

Selling to problems only reaches the small portion already shopping for your type of product.

→

Desires

Explain motivation — what the customer wants to achieve or become.

Desires determine the creative direction. They explain why someone would stop scrolling. They decide what to build.

Selling to desires opens your total addressable market.

Key Insight: If your ads agitate pain but don't close the loop with a desirable outcome, the viewer resonates but scrolls away in confusion. Every creative must clearly activate one desire and show the transformation.

What Are Mass Desires?

A mass desire is a want that already exists and is shared by a large group of people. It's not created by your product — it exists independently in the market. Your product just fulfills it.

Example: WildOne (Waterproof Pet Walk Kit)

💫
Aesthetic Identity

"I want gear that reflects my style and standards"

💪
Public Confidence

"I want to feel in control when people are watching"

🙈
Embarrassment Avoidance

"I want to stop dreading walks in public"

None of these desires are created by a leash product. They already exist in the market. Understanding them lets you speak to what actually motivates purchases.

Why Markets Are Emotionally Fragmented

Different people buy for different reasons. What motivates one buyer is different from what motivates another, even for the exact same product.

This is why you need 5 mass desires, not 1. Relying on a single desire limits scale and causes creative fatigue. When you find a winning ad, you've often only unlocked a small portion of your addressable market. Five desires give you five entry points into the same market.

Rule: Every ad must clearly belong to one desire bucket. If you mix multiple desires in one ad, you won't know which message resonated and you can't replicate success.

3

Angles — Stories That Trigger Desires

What Is an Angle?

An angle is one specific story that activates a desire in a recognizable situation. It represents a moment of realization where the viewer emotionally connects.

Angles are NOT hooks or headlines. They define the entire creative idea. The angle is the story; the hook is just the first line that pulls someone in.

Example: 5 Angles for "Stop Being Physically Overpowered" Desire

Angle 1
"The Invisible Injury"

She's been walking her Labrador for 3 years. The walks work. But her right shoulder has been aching for months. At the physiotherapist, she realizes something she did every day was quietly injuring her.

Angle 2
"The Morning Dread"

She used to love morning walks. Now she wakes up calculating whether she has enough arm strength today. The dog feels her tension. Both of them are anxious before they even leave the door.

Angle 3
"The Public Scene"

Another dog walker approaches on the trail. Her dog surges forward. She's dragged 3 steps before catching her balance. The other person gives her "that look." She decides to take shorter routes where no one can see.

Angle 4
"The Lifestyle Thief"

She used to hike every weekend. Now she makes excuses. The dog is too strong on uneven terrain. She's lost a hobby she loved — not because of her body, but because of her equipment.

Angle 5
"The Breaking Point"

It happened fast. A squirrel, a hard lunge, a wrist that folded the wrong way. She sat on the sidewalk holding her wrist, tears from pain and frustration. This was the same walk she's done 1,000 times.

Why 5 Angles Per Desire?

  • The same desire resonates through different real-life situations
  • Different stories activate the same desire for different people
  • 5 angles explore different contexts, fears, and moments of realization
  • It reveals which story patterns consistently perform (not just one-off spikes)
Patterns are what allow you to scale confidently. A single winning ad is luck. Three winning ads from the same desire is a pattern you can build on.
Each angle must unlock a transformation. Desires are deep emotional states people resonate with but can't always define themselves. The angle is what they recognize — the painful moment, the story, the narrative. But the transformation happens at the desire level.

Awareness Levels

The 5 Levels of Customer Awareness

Awareness determines how much explanation and context your creative needs — not what creative idea to test.

UNAWARE Doesn't know the problem exists yet
PROBLEM AWARE Feels pain, doesn't know solutions exist
SOLUTION AWARE Knows options exist out there
PRODUCT AWARE Knows your brand/product exists
MOST AWARE Ready to buy (~3% of market)
Top of funnel (Unaware / Problem Aware): Your creative needs more context, more storytelling, more education. You must take them on a journey. The ad + landing page must move them through multiple awareness stages.
Bottom of funnel (Product / Most Aware): Your creative can be direct. They already know the problem and the solution. Give them the information they need to make the purchase decision right now.

Awareness in Creative Strategy

Concept Decides... Example
Desires WHAT to test (creative direction) "Test the aesthetic identity desire bucket"
Awareness HOW directly to say it "This audience is unaware, so lead with story not product"
Angles Which STORY to tell "Use the invisible injury narrative"
Mechanism WHY to believe the promise "Redistributes force through geometry, not grip strength"
People don't need time to make a purchasing decision. They need information. If you want them to buy right then, give them all the information they require. The higher up the awareness ladder, the more information your creative and sales funnel needs.
5

The Unique Mechanism

What Makes Your Promise Believable

The unique mechanism is the reason your promise is believable. It explains why your product works differently — not what the creative idea is, but why the claims in the creative are justified.

Customer thinks:
"I've tried everything
and nothing worked"
→
Mechanism shows:
"Oh, THAT's why
nothing worked"
→
Customer believes:
"This is different.
This could work."
Example (WildOne): "The problem is cumulative physical trauma from asymmetrical load — same arm, same wrist. The solution redirects the dog's movement through geometry rather than the owner's grip strength, so the body absorbs near-zero impact regardless of what the dog does."

The Critical Rule: Keep Mechanism Consistent

When testing desires and angles, keep the mechanism the same across all 25 creatives.

What You're Testing (Variables)

  • Mass Desires — the psychological reason people buy
  • Angles — the story you tell to trigger action

What You Keep Constant (Controls)

  • Mechanism — the rationale and justification
  • Ad format — keep consistent for clean comparison
This prevents confusing creative success with mechanical differences. Clean testing = clear scaling decisions.

Generating the 25 Ads (Step by Step)

Phase A: Market Research with AI

Use AI (Claude recommended with Opus model, or ChatGPT) to systematically uncover your market's emotional landscape.

1

Prompt 1: Market Research

Input your product, niche, and primary competitor URL. Enable web search. The AI will research the emotional landscape, your competitor's customer base, and identify underlying problems.

  • Excludes brand/product complaints — focuses on the problem space
  • Prioritizes detailed emotional narratives over brief mentions
  • Identifies internal emotional states, language patterns, and customer vocabulary
2

Prompt 2: Deep Forum Analysis

The AI analyzes 20+ public discussions (predominantly Reddit). It extracts:

Language Patterns
20-30 commonly used phrases, recurring words, demographic jargon
Emotional Mapping
Primary emotions, intensity levels, emotional progression patterns
Belief Systems
Necessary belief shifts to convert them into a customer
3

Prompt 3: AI Copywriter Training

Train the AI with copywriting skills, unique mechanism frameworks, and your brand's foundational docs. This prepares it for strategic output in the next steps.

4

Prompt 4: Generate 5 Mass Desires

Attach your foundational docs and customer avatar. The AI produces 5 distinct mass desires based on all the research. Copy each desire into your tracking spreadsheet.

Read every output carefully. Don't just copy-paste. You need to understand your customers' deep desires to be an effective creative strategist. The AI is your assistant, not your replacement.
5

Prompt 5: Generate 5 Angles Per Desire

Run this prompt 5 times — once for each mass desire. Each run produces 5 angles with full detail:

  • Angle name (becomes your headline concept)
  • Target audience for this specific angle
  • Story arc — the narrative the viewer will connect with
  • Unique problem mechanism — why nothing else worked
  • Unique solution mechanism — why this is different
  • Key emotional moment — the turning point in the story

Copy each angle into your spreadsheet. Save the full detail to a Google Doc — you'll need it when generating ads.

Phase B: Generating Image Ads

For initial testing, use static image ads to identify which desires and angles convert. This is fast and efficient with AI.

1

Prepare Your Inputs

  • Clean, high-resolution product photo on plain white background
  • A competitor image ad you want to model (style reference)
  • Your mass desire + angle detail (from the Google Doc)
2

Customize the Image Generation Prompt

Use the image generation prompt template. Insert:

  • Headline: from your angle's key emotional moment or angle name
  • Desire: the mass desire this ad belongs to

Upload product photo + competitor reference ad along with the prompt to Claude.

3

Generate in Image Model

Take the output prompt from Claude and paste it into your image generation tool (recommended: Google NanoBanana Pro). Attach product photo and competitor reference. Generate the image.

4

Repeat 25 Times

Go back to your spreadsheet. Move to the next angle. Update the headline and desire in the prompt. Generate. Repeat until all 25 are done.

Headlines & Primary Text: Use the headline/copy prompting templates (linked in the Google Doc). They have different tabs for top-of-funnel and bottom-of-funnel. Attach your foundational docs, the desire, and the angle — and the AI generates primary text and headlines tailored to the awareness level.

Tracking Your Creative Strategy

The Ad Tracking System

Build a spreadsheet (or use the template linked with the course) that tracks each creative with context.

Column What to Track Why
Mass Desire Which of the 5 desires this ad targets Identify which desire buckets are strong
Angle The specific story/narrative used See which stories resonate within a desire
Awareness Level Top / Mid / Bottom funnel Know what copy style and context to use
Headline The headline used in the ad Quick reference for iteration
Primary Text The ad copy Compare copy approaches
Creative File Link or image reference Visual tracking across all 25
Winner? Yes / No / Promising At-a-glance pattern recognition
Key Metrics CTR, CPC, ROAS, CVR Performance comparison
Give each ad an identifiable unique name that includes the desire number and angle number (e.g., "D1-A3-InvisibleInjury"). This makes it easy to track in Ads Manager and in your spreadsheet.

How to Read Your Results

At the Desire Level (Ad Set)

Look across all 5 angles within each desire:

  • 3+ winning angles in a desire = this desire is strong, double down
  • 1-2 winning angles = desire may work, expand with more angles first
  • 0 winning angles = desire is likely weak for this market, not your execution

At the Angle Level (Ad)

Compare within and across desires:

  • Which story patterns consistently perform?
  • Is it top-of-funnel or bottom-of-funnel that converts better?
  • Which emotional moments get the highest CTR?
  • Do certain angle types work across multiple desires?

Iteration — Where Scale Actually Happens

Why Iteration Matters More Than Initial Testing

Initial testing only reveals which desires resonate — not just which ads win. Scaling comes from repeating what works across multiple angles. Iteration is about expanding proven ideas, not chasing new ones.

If you're continuously testing creatives but not finding new winners: You probably haven't understood what desire made your customer convert and what angle told the story that triggered it. Without understanding that, you can't repeat it.

The 80/20 Budget Rule

Once you have initial results from your 25-creative test:

80% — Iterations of Winners
20% — New Tests

80% → Iteration

  • New angles around winning desires
  • New story variations of winning angles
  • Different formats of proven concepts (video, carousel, UGC)
  • Refined copy/headlines on winning visuals

20% → New Testing

  • New angles under existing desires
  • Completely new desires (if the first 5 are exhausted)
  • New formats or platforms
  • Wild card creative experiments
