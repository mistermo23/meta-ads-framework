The CRO Foundation

Why CRO Matters More Than More Traffic

Most brands throw money at ads to get more visitors. The profitable brands fix the website first so every visitor is worth more. A 1% increase in conversion rate on a store doing $100K/month can mean $25K+ more per year — without spending a single extra dollar on ads.

The core truth: Your ad campaign's ROAS ceiling is set by your website's conversion rate. The best ad in the world sends traffic to a page that either converts or doesn't. CRO is what unlocks ad profitability.

The 6 Core Principles

95% Subconscious

Purchase decisions are made by System 1 — fast, automatic, emotional. Design and copy must win the implicit judgment before rational thinking begins.

Emotions Sell, Logic Justifies

Lead with emotional triggers. Give facts after the decision has been made emotionally so the buyer can rationalize it to themselves and others.

Sell the Transformation

Never sell the product. Always sell what owning it changes about their life, confidence, relationships, or status. The product behind the product.

Keep Visitors in System 1

Any friction, confusion, or unanswered question pushes visitors into System 2 critical thinking and kills conversion. Cognitive ease is your friend.

Research Before Writing

Customer surveys, reviews, and voice-of-customer data must inform every word. Copy based on assumptions fails. Copy based on exact customer language converts.

FURs Framework

Every piece of copy must address at least one Fear, Uncertainty, or Risk of the target buyer. If you're not resolving a FUR, you're not converting.

System 1 vs System 2 Thinking

System 1 — Converts

  • Fast, automatic, emotional
  • Drives 95% of purchase decisions
  • Responds to familiar design patterns
  • Activated by simple copy and clear visuals
  • Feels effortless — visitor stays engaged
vs

System 2 — Kills Conversion

  • Slow, rational, deliberate
  • Activates when something feels "off"
  • Triggered by confusion or friction
  • Makes visitors question, compare, hesitate
  • High energy — visitor looks for exit

Consumer Psychology Triggers

Attention & Cognitive Ease

The first 5 seconds decide everything. Above the fold must instantly communicate what it is, who it's for, and why it matters.

  • Cognitive Ease: Familiar design, short sentences, 5th-grade reading level, no contradictory info. More minimalist = more trust.
  • Curiosity Gap: Hint at surprising outcome without revealing it. Only works if the reader has baseline knowledge of the topic.
  • Dopamine Trigger: Releases when visitor connects the offer to a real-life situation they recognize. Use storytelling and specific scenarios.
  • Benefit Repetition: Repeat 2–3 core benefits throughout the page using different sentences. Mere exposure effect compounds trust.

Loss Aversion & Scarcity

Losing something hurts twice as much as gaining the same thing feels good. This is the most powerful purchase motivator available.

Frame as Loss

"Don't miss your $5 saving" — frames the discount as something they'll lose if they don't act.

Scarcity Tactics

Stock bars ("Only 12% remaining"), countdown timers, inventory messages ("3 left, 20 people viewing"). Don't fake it — that destroys trust.

Coupon code warning: A visible empty coupon field sends people to Google to find a code. Hide it by default. Only show when a discount is active or as a collapsed accordion.

Social Proof & Trust

  • Cheerleader Effect: Show multiple testimonial faces together, walls of reviews, not single isolated reviews. Objects appear more attractive in groups.
  • Single Detailed Testimonial: One emotional 5-star review with full name, photo, and specific outcome beats 25 generic reviews.
  • Allow Negative Reviews: A 4.5 average is more credible than 5.0. Perfect scores signal fake.
  • Base Rate Reframing: Use the most favorable base. "99% of active customers give us 5 stars" beats "80% of all customers rate us highly."
  • Mere Exposure Effect: Show the product repeatedly throughout the page — hero, lifestyle shots, video, use-case sections, upsell, footer. Each view increases positive perception.
  • Trust Badges: Payment logos (PayPal, Visa, Mastercard, Klarna) in footer, product page, cart drawer, and checkout.

Pricing Psychology

Price Anchor

First number seen becomes the reference. Show crossed-out original price before sale price. Any large number nearby works as anchor.

Charm Pricing

Prices ending in 9 increase sales ~24%. $1,499 feels dramatically cheaper than $1,500. Exception: luxury uses round numbers.

Price Formatting

Write $89 not $89.00. Shorter representation feels smaller. For subscriptions, use price sandwich: "$1.50/day" beats "$547/year."

Decoy Effect

Three-tier pricing: middle option pre-selected and badged "Most Popular." Bottom intentionally unattractive, top extravagant. Middle wins.

Magnitude Priming

Unrelated large numbers near the price make it feel smaller. "869 reviews" displayed larger than "$89" makes the price shrink perceptually.

Veblen Effect

For status goods, higher price increases demand. Higher price = higher perceived quality. Price higher and tell a compelling quality story.

Convincing & Closing Triggers

  • Foot in the Door: Small commitment leads to bigger commitment. Email opt-in → tripwire → core offer → upsell. Each micro-conversion increases the next.
  • Door in the Face: Lead with outrageous ask, follow with real ask which now feels reasonable. Premium bundle at top, individual items feel like bargains below.
  • Yes Street: Chain 3–4 yes questions targeting specific problems, then place the CTA immediately after. Works in copy, email, and advertorials.
  • Commitment & Consistency: Once someone makes a small decision, they seek reasons to stay consistent. "Learn more" as first CTA beats "Buy now" for cold audiences.
  • Diderot Effect: After buying, customers want complementary products to complete the set. The highest-leverage cross-sell moment is immediately post-purchase.
  • Post-Decision Rationalization: After buying, send a founder email praising the purchase and reminding them of benefits. Reduces returns and builds loyalty before the product arrives.

The 4 Buyer Personality Types

The Motive Compass

Every audience maps to one or more personality types driven by core hormones. Identifying the dominant type determines tone, language, visuals, and copy approach for everything you produce. The stronger you stay in one positioning, the higher the conversion.

How to identify your audience: (1) Look at the product category. (2) Mine customer language from reviews, surveys, and emails. (3) Look at competitor ads and what tone is winning.
Blue — The Thinker

Cortisol | Security & Reason

Analytical, skeptical, data-driven. Needs all facts before buying. Engineers, researchers, finance types.

Marketing Rules

  • Zero hype, zero exaggeration, serious tone
  • Lead with numbers, data, evidence, guarantees
  • Longer detailed text is fine — they will read it
  • Away-from motivation: risks eliminated, losses prevented
  • Never use lowball pricing or hidden costs

CTA style: "Analyze your options" • "See all specifications" • "Get the complete guide"

Green — The Keeper

Oxytocin | Connection & Harmony

Relationship-driven, empathetic, values-led. Environmentalists, parents, community builders.

Marketing Rules

  • Soft marketing, zero pressure, zero fear tactics
  • Address them personally and warmly
  • Build trust through relationship before asking
  • Focus on people, not objects
  • Never use scarcity or aggressive urgency

CTA style: "Join our community" • "Start your journey" • "Help us make a difference"

Red — The Doer

Testosterone | Status & Results

Action-oriented, competitive, status-driven. Entrepreneurs, athletes, high achievers.

Marketing Rules

  • Direct, tougher marketing — get to the point
  • Towards motivation: gains, achievements, results
  • Use scarcity and urgency aggressively
  • Focus on objects, status symbols, outcomes
  • Give choices between packages, never binary

CTA style: "Get started now" • "Secure your spot" • "Claim your results"

Yellow — The Entertainer

Dopamine | Freedom & Creativity

Fun-loving, spontaneous, expressive. Artists, travelers, influencers, creatives.

Marketing Rules

  • Easy, fun, light — never heavy or serious
  • Conversational tone, emojis OK, lots of color
  • Towards motivation: possibilities and freedom
  • Focus on people and experiences over objects
  • Emphasize uniqueness and self-expression

CTA style: "Do your thing" • "Start exploring" • "Live your best life now"

The 5 Awareness Stages

Match Copy to Where the Traffic Actually Is

Running Stage 4 copy to cold Stage 1 traffic is one of the most expensive mistakes in ecommerce. Every traffic source has an awareness level — your copy must meet them there.

The #1 CRO mistake: Writing product-aware copy (stage 4) for cold Meta ad traffic that's only at stage 1–2. The visitor doesn't even know they have a problem yet, and you're showing them a product page.
Stage 1: Unaware Doesn't know they have a problem
Stage 2: Problem Aware Knows the problem, not the solution
Stage 3: Solution Aware Knows solutions exist, not yours
Stage 4: Product Aware Knows your product, hasn't bought
Stage 5: Most Aware Has tried & failed, highest skepticism

Stage-by-Stage Copy Approach

Stage Traffic Type Copy Approach Example Opening
1. Unaware Cold top of funnel Hypnotic speech that gently surfaces the problem "Maybe you know this too..."
2. Problem Aware Cold awareness Lead with possibility — open the door "Yes, it is actually possible to..."
3. Solution Aware Warm, researching Give the mechanism and steps "3 steps to finally fix..."
4. Product Aware Retargeting, email Remove friction, offer support "I will help you implement this"
5. Most Aware Previous customers Address failed attempts, new mechanism "Have you tried X but couldn't stick?"

Copywriting Frameworks

The 5 Core Formulas

PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve

Name the exact pain. Go deeper — connect to identity, emotions, consequences. Then present the product as the inevitable answer.

Best for: emotional products with clear pain, cold traffic

AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

Stop them with a bold claim. Deepen curiosity. Connect solution to their want. Clear direct CTA.

Best for: general purpose, works across most formats

BAB — Before, After, Bridge

Describe the painful current situation. Paint the desired future. Show how the product bridges the gap.

Best for: transformation products, clean narrative

4Ps — Picture, Promise, Proof, Push

Paint a vivid scene. Make a bold guarantee. Back it with testimonials and data. Aggressive CTA with urgency.

Best for: high-ticket, trust-dependent offers

Headline Rules

  • Lead with biggest benefit or sharpest pain — never with product name
  • Specific beats vague: "Fix the one thing making your room look cheap" beats "Upgrade your home"
  • Under 10 words ideal for above the fold
  • Never start with "we" or "our" — start with "you," a question, or a provocative statement
  • Headline and hero image must tell the same story
  • Match headline tone to buyer personality type

Headline Framework Examples

Curiosity Gap
"She almost returned it. Then she slept on it one night."
Problem Trance
"Tired of rugs that slide, bunch, and ruin your floors?"
Transformation
"From chaotic room to designer space. One rug changes everything."
Ego Labeling
"For homeowners who refuse to settle for almost."
Yes Street
"Have you ever walked into a room and immediately felt calm?"
Social Proof
"9,000 customers finally fixed the one thing their room was missing."

CTA System

The CTA is the conversion moment. Must be direct, elegant, and completely free of doubt. No ifs, no whens, no maybes. Never just say "buy now" or "submit."

Weak CTAs
  • "Submit"
  • "Buy now"
  • "Click here"
  • "Learn more" (as primary CTA)
Strong CTAs
  • "Add to Cart — Ships Tomorrow"
  • "Yes, I want to transform my skin"
  • "Save $X by enrolling now"
  • "Secure your spot — only 3 left"

Common Copywriting Mistakes

  • Writing for yourself: Always ask "what's in it for them?"
  • Features over benefits: Take one step further — what does this feature mean for their life?
  • Too complicated: 5th-grade reading level. Max 15–20 words per sentence.
  • Wrong personality type: Mismatch between copy tone and audience type kills conversion instantly.
  • No tension: Monotone copy loses attention. Vary sentence length and energy.
  • Writing before researching: Customer language first, copy second. Always.
Weak words to cut: help, try, believe, could, maybe, potentially. And: overusing I, we, me. The customer doesn't care about you.

UX & Design Laws

The 6 Gestalt Laws of Conversion Design

Visitors get a whole feeling instantly, then zoom in on individual elements afterward. There is no middle ground — layouts are perceived as harmonious or inharmonious at first glance.

1. Figure & Ground

Important elements sit visually forward. Add box shadows to CTAs. High contrast between product and background.

2. Similarity

One CTA color, one shape, one size — everywhere across every page. Consistent design trains visitors to navigate without thinking.

3. Proximity

Elements placed closer together are perceived as belonging together. Group price, rating, and CTA tightly. White space signals where groups end.

4. Unity / Closure

Use alternating backgrounds to signal sections. Never mix content types within a visual zone. Above the fold = one complete impression.

5. Continuity

Eye follows lines and flows. Page should create a natural journey: headline → benefits → proof → CTA. Progress bars guide momentum.

6. Common Destiny

Elements that move together = one group. Hover animations, accordions, progressive disclosure. Show only what's needed at each moment.

Image Psychology

  • Show product in use: Customers instantly imagine themselves using it. Lifestyle beats sterile white backgrounds.
  • People smiling: Happy expressions transfer positive emotion unconsciously. Genuine expressions outperform posed shots.
  • Left-to-right scanning: Product on the left, CTA and details on the right. Work with the natural Western reading pattern.
  • Golden ratio positioning: Don't center the product exactly. Position in the left or right third for visual tension.
  • Contrast with background: Dark product on light background or vice versa. Without contrast the product gets lost.
  • Bokeh effect: Blur the background to isolate the product. Creates premium aesthetic, directs full attention to the product.
  • Reveal, don't conceal: Show the product doing what it does. Visual proof of the benefit beats any claim in copy.
  • Vampire effect warning: Models too attractive or revealing steal attention from the product. Models should enhance, not overshadow.

Key UX Principles

  • Hick's Law: More choices = longer decision = higher abandonment. Max 5 product variants. Break complex decisions into steps.
  • First Impression Rule: You cannot reason your way out of a bad first impression. The subconscious verdict is made in milliseconds.
  • Minimalism: The more minimalist a homepage, the more cognitive ease and trust it promotes. Remove anything that doesn't serve conversion.
  • Progress Psychology: Progress bars reduce abandonment. People have a strong drive to finish things they've started (Zeigarnik effect).
  • Don't make them do math: Show old price, new price, and exact savings. Never make the customer calculate anything.

Homepage

Goal: Capture attention, communicate positioning, qualify the visitor, and direct them to the right product in seconds.

Above the Fold (The 5-Second Test)

  • Headline: biggest benefit or sharpest problem, never product or brand name
  • Subheadline: expand on the promise, add specificity or social proof
  • Hero image: product in use or the transformation — lifestyle always beats product-only
  • Primary CTA visible without scrolling — "Shop [Collection]" beats "Buy Now" for cold traffic
  • Announcement bar: free shipping threshold, current offer, or core brand USP

Below the Fold

  • Trust signals: customer count, review count, years in business, press logos
  • 3 benefit icons with short explanations
  • Yes Street sequence: 3–4 yes questions followed by CTA
  • Testimonial wall with photos, names, specific outcomes
  • Collection or product showcase sorted by bestselling (never by newest or cheapest)

Collection Page

Goal: Help visitors find the right product quickly and build confidence before clicking through.

  • Clear category structure with intuitive subcategories
  • Visible filtering and sorting — default: best selling or relevance
  • Never show cheapest products first (ruins price anchor)
  • Product cards: lifestyle image primary, star rating, review count, "Best Seller" badge on top performers
  • Collection headline with ego labeling: "For homeowners who care what their home feels like"

Product Detail Page (PDP)

Goal: Build desire, answer every question, eliminate every objection, and close the sale. The PDP is a complete sales argument — nothing is decoration.

Hero Section

  • Images: lifestyle first, product-only second, in-use third, detail fourth
  • Show product from multiple angles including being used by a person
  • Product title: benefit-driven, not technical code
  • Star rating and review count immediately below title
  • 3–5 benefit bullets hitting core FURs

Pricing & Bundles

  • Anchor price crossed out, then sale price. Show dollar amount saved.
  • Max 3–5 variants (Hick's Law). Bundles in 3 tiers with middle pre-selected.
  • Scarcity indicator: stock level, people viewing, units remaining

Add to Cart Zone

  • CTA contrasts sharply with page (figure/ground law) with box shadow
  • CTA with meaning: "Add to Cart — Ships Tomorrow"
  • Trust badges + payment logos adjacent to CTA
  • Express checkout (Shop Pay, Apple Pay, PayPal) directly beneath

Below-the-Fold Sections

  • Description: Feature → Benefit → FUR format. Lead with transformation.
  • Reviews: Filterable, with photos, names, specific outcomes. Feature one detailed testimonial.
  • FAQ: 5–7 most common pre-purchase objections as accordion.
  • Cross-sells: "Complete the set" framing, max 3–4 products.
  • Sticky bar: Appears after original CTA scrolls out of view.

Cart, Checkout & Thank You

Cart Drawer Optimization

  • Trust reinforcement: "Free shipping over $X," "30-day returns," "Secure checkout" visible in cart
  • Commitment message: "Great choice — your order ships tomorrow" (commitment & consistency principle)
  • One cross-sell max: "Customers who bought this also added..." (Diderot effect)
  • Progress bar: "You're $X away from free shipping" with visual bar (gamification + AOV driver)
  • Coupon field: Hidden by default. Only show when discount is active.
  • Express checkout: Shop Pay, Apple Pay, PayPal above main checkout button

Top Checkout Abandonment Reasons

Reason % of Abandonment Fix
Unexpected additional costs 48% Show all costs before checkout — on PDP and in cart
Mandatory account registration 25% Always offer guest checkout, never force signup
Delivery not fast enough — Show estimated delivery on product page and cart
Lack of trust / missing payment logos — Payment logos everywhere, store benefits in checkout
Checkout too complex — Follow standard Shopify conventions, reduce fields
Unsatisfactory returns policy — State 30-day guarantee, free returns, 7-day refund clearly

Checkout Optimization Tactics

  • Progress bar at top: Address → Shipping → Payment
  • Combine first and last name into one field
  • Enable autocomplete for address fields
  • Inline validation: green checkmark on correct, red highlight on errors in real time
  • Store benefit reminders inside checkout: guarantee, free shipping, easy returns
  • Display all payment method logos
  • Hide coupon field unless a code is active

Thank You Page

Goal: Reinforce the decision, prevent buyer's remorse, plant the seed for repeat purchase.

  • Confirmation: Clear warm message, order summary with images, estimated delivery date
  • Post-decision praise: "You just made a great choice" + remind them of 2–3 benefits they'll experience
  • Cross-sell: "Complete your experience" — this is the highest-conversion cross-sell moment in the entire funnel
  • Community: Welcome to the customer base, invite social sharing or referral
  • Referral seed: "Share with a friend and you both get X"

How CRO Connects to Ad Campaigns

The Ads ↔ CRO Feedback Loop

Your ad campaign and your website are not separate systems. They are one continuous persuasion sequence. The ad makes a promise. The website delivers on it. If there's a disconnect, you lose the visitor instantly.

The golden rule: Ad creative, landing page, and checkout must all tell the same story. Inconsistency between what the ad promises and what the page delivers is the single biggest ROAS killer.

Why CRO Unlocks Ad Profitability

Before CRO

1,000 visitors × 1.5% CVR = 15 orders
$50 AOV = $750 revenue
$500 ad spend = 1.5x ROAS

After CRO

1,000 visitors × 3% CVR = 30 orders
$60 AOV (bundles) = $1,800 revenue
$500 ad spend = 3.6x ROAS

Same traffic, same ad spend. CRO doubled the conversion rate and AOV improvements added another 40%. The result is a 2.4x improvement in ROAS without touching a single ad.

Matching Awareness Stages to Traffic Sources

Traffic Source Awareness Level Landing Page Strategy
Cold Meta prospecting ads Stage 1–2 Advertorial or listicle → warm them up before PDP
Meta retargeting ads Stage 3–4 Direct to PDP with social proof and urgency
Google branded search Stage 4 Direct to PDP — they already know you
Google non-branded search Stage 2–3 Collection page or PDP with education angle
Email campaigns Stage 4–5 Direct to offer page with exclusive incentive
Influencer / UGC links Stage 2–3 Dedicated landing page matching the influencer's messaging

Ad-to-Page Congruence Checklist

  • Visual congruence: Hero image on landing page should match or complement the ad creative. Same color palette, same aesthetic.
  • Message congruence: Headline on landing page should echo the ad's promise. If the ad says "Fix your posture in 2 weeks," the page headline should reinforce that exact claim.
  • Personality type match: If the ad targets Red types with urgency and status, the landing page can't switch to soft Green language. Stay in the same positioning field.
  • Awareness stage match: Cold traffic ads should land on education pages (advertorials), not product pages. Product-aware retargeting should land on PDPs with urgency.
  • Offer match: If the ad mentions a discount, the discount must be visible immediately on the landing page. No hunting, no confusion.

The Advertorial Bridge

For cold Meta traffic (Stage 1–2), sending directly to a PDP is usually inefficient. The advertorial sits between the ad and the product page, warming up the visitor:

Cold Ad
→
Advertorial
→
PDP
→
Checkout

The advertorial takes the visitor from Stage 1–2 to Stage 3–4 using storytelling (PAS or Hero's Journey), social proof, and education. By the time they reach the PDP, they're warm and ready to buy.

When to use an advertorial: When your ad audience is cold (doesn't know they have a problem or doesn't know your product exists), when average order value is above $50, or when the product requires explanation or trust-building.

Using CRO Data to Improve Ads

  • Heatmaps reveal hooks: Where visitors spend the most time on your page tells you what messages resonate. Turn those into ad angles.
  • Top FAQ questions = ad objections: Whatever your FAQ answers most, your ads should proactively address.
  • Review language = ad copy: The exact words customers use in reviews are the most persuasive language for your ads.
  • Best-performing PDP sections = ad creative direction: If the transformation section gets the most engagement, lead your ads with transformation messaging.
  • Checkout drop-off data = trust gaps: If visitors abandon at shipping costs, your ads should mention "free shipping" upfront.

  • Advertorial used for cold traffic when needed
  • Offer visible immediately on landing page

Design Team Guidelines

Mobile Above-the-Fold Dimensions

The "above the fold" on mobile is the visible area before any scrolling. Your hero section must communicate value, show the product, and present a CTA within this space.

Device Viewport Width Above-the-Fold Height Notes
iPhone SE / small 375px ~550px Tightest constraint — design for this first
iPhone 14 / 15 390px ~660px Most common iOS device class
iPhone 14 Pro Max / 15 Plus 430px ~740px Larger phones give more breathing room
Android mid-range 360–412px ~600–700px Wide variance — test on Samsung Galaxy
Desktop 1280–1440px ~700–800px Design for 1440px wide, test at 1280px
Design rule: Browser chrome (address bar, nav) eats ~100–130px on mobile. Your "safe zone" for must-see content on iPhone 14 is roughly 390 × 660px. Always test real devices, not just browser resize.

What Must Be Above the Fold (Mobile)

1

Headline + Subheadline

The headline must communicate what you sell, who it's for, and why they should care. Budget roughly 80–120px for headline and sub.

2

Hero Image or Video

Lifestyle shot showing product in use. Recommended hero image height on mobile: 300–400px. Don't make it so tall that CTA gets pushed below fold.

3

Primary CTA

Must be fully visible without scrolling. On mobile this usually means overlaying or placing CTA directly below the hero image. Full-width button preferred on mobile.

4

Trust Signal (Optional but Recommended)

A single line like "Trusted by 10,000+ customers" or a star rating. Even a small trust element above the fold increases scroll depth.

Typography & Readability Rules

Font Sizes

  • Body text: 16px minimum on mobile (never 14px or smaller)
  • H1 (hero): 28–36px mobile, 40–56px desktop
  • H2 (section): 22–28px mobile, 32–40px desktop
  • H3 (card): 18–22px mobile, 20–24px desktop
  • Captions/labels: 12–14px, use sparingly
  • CTA buttons: 16–18px, bold weight

Line Length & Spacing

  • Max line length: 65–75 characters per line (including spaces)
  • Line height: 1.5–1.7 for body copy
  • Paragraph spacing: 1rem–1.5rem between paragraphs
  • Letter spacing: 0 for body, 0.03–0.05em for uppercase labels
  • Max 2 font families: One for headings, one for body
  • Contrast ratio: 4.5:1 minimum for body text (WCAG AA)

CTA Button Specifications

  • Min height: 48px on mobile (Apple & Google touch target guidelines), 44px on desktop
  • Min width: Full-width on mobile (100% of container), min 200px on desktop
  • Padding: 14–18px vertical, 24–32px horizontal
  • Border radius: 8–12px for modern feel, or match brand style
  • Box shadow: Always add subtle shadow to lift CTA off the page (figure/ground law)
  • One CTA color everywhere: Same color, same border radius, same shadow on every page — homepage, PDP, collection, cart, checkout
  • Contrast: CTA must be the highest-contrast element on the page. If your page is white/light, use a bold dark or saturated button color

Don't

  • Blue CTA on homepage, green on PDP, red in cart
  • Ghost/outline buttons as primary CTA
  • CTA that blends with the page background
  • Multiple competing CTA styles on one page
vs

Do

  • One bold CTA color site-wide
  • Solid filled buttons with box shadow
  • High contrast against page background
  • Consistent sizing and corner radius

Image & Asset Requirements

Hero Images
  • Desktop: 1440 × 600–800px (2x for retina: 2880px wide)
  • Mobile: 750 × 800–1000px (2x for retina)
  • Format: WebP with JPEG fallback
  • File size: under 200KB compressed
  • Always include alt text
Product Images
  • Minimum: 1000 × 1000px (Shopify zoom requirement)
  • Recommended: 2048 × 2048px square
  • Consistent aspect ratio across all products
  • First image: lifestyle. Second: product-only. Third: in-use. Fourth: detail.
  • Background: lifestyle or clean neutral (not pure white)

Spacing & Layout System

  • Use an 8px grid: All spacing values should be multiples of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 80, 96). Creates visual rhythm and consistency.
  • Section padding: 64–96px vertical on desktop, 48–64px on mobile
  • Card padding: 24–32px on desktop, 16–24px on mobile
  • Content max-width: 1200–1440px for main container. Center with auto margins.
  • White space is structural: It signals where one group ends and another begins. It is not wasted space — it prevents cognitive overload.
  • Alternating section backgrounds: Use subtle background color changes between sections to create visual separation (Gestalt unity/closure law).

Mobile-Specific Design Rules

  • Touch targets: All tappable elements minimum 48 × 48px with 8px gap between targets
  • No hover-only interactions: Everything must work with tap. Hover tooltips need tap alternatives.
  • Sticky add-to-cart bar: 56–64px tall, fixed to bottom of viewport, appears after main CTA scrolls out of view
  • Thumb zone: Primary actions (CTA, navigation) should be in the bottom 40% of the screen where thumbs reach easily
  • Image carousels: Use swipe indicators (dots). Limit to 5–7 images max. Lazy load after first 2.
  • Accordions over tabs: On mobile, vertical accordions for FAQs, product details, and shipping info are easier to use than horizontal tabs
  • Full-width buttons: All primary CTAs should be full-width (100%) on mobile. Never side-by-side CTA buttons on mobile.
  • No horizontal scroll: Nothing on the page should require horizontal scrolling except product image carousels and comparison tables

Color & Contrast Checklist

  • Primary brand color: Used for CTAs, links, active states. Must have 4.5:1 contrast against white.
  • Success state: Green (#10B981 or similar) for confirmations, checkmarks, positive feedback
  • Error state: Red (#EF4444 or similar) for form errors, warnings, out-of-stock
  • Sale/discount: Red or orange for crossed-out prices and sale badges (pattern recognition)
  • Neutral palette: 4–5 grays from near-black to near-white for text hierarchy and backgrounds
  • Never use color alone: Pair color with icons, text, or shape changes for accessibility
